## 3. Forward guidance

Q1'26 revenue guide of $222M–$232M (midpoint $227M) signals continued strength right out of the gate — sequentially flat to modestly up versus a $222.6M Q4'25 print, but on a Y/Y basis a meaningful acceleration. Management explicitly framed the shape of 2026 as H1 stronger than H1'25, with acceleration in H2. Full-year framing is qualitative rather than a point estimate: "another growth year," "outperform WFE," with WFE pegged at low-double-digit growth, implying Nova revenue growth comfortably above mid-teens.

Q1 gross margin guide is "slightly lower" than Q4, attributed entirely to product mix (CFO Kizner in Q&A) — explicitly not a structural margin compression. Operating margin model remains the 30%+ non-GAAP zone given FY25 ran at 33%.

Guidance philosophy unchanged from prior quarters: point estimate for the upcoming quarter (revenue range, EPS guide of $2.11 was the Q4 prior guide), qualitative directional commentary for the full year. No shift toward more conservative or more ambitious framing — confident but not aggressive.

## 4. Thesis read by theme

### foundry_capacity

- **Status:** Confirm
- **Evidence:** Management described "strong positioning across all four leading-edge logic players" and confirmed adoption "by a leading logic customer for gate-all-around processes" (prepared remarks / Shane Brett Q&A, Morgan Stanley). Customer engagement spans all major foundries, with the Elizabeth/Citi Q&A confirming Nova is "on track to meet its gate-all-around revenue target" of cumulative $500M+ by 2026. Geographic mix shows Taiwan grew nearly 90% Y/Y, now 29% of revenue — direct read on foundry capacity buildout at the leading-edge node transition.
- **Implication:** The foundry capacity thesis for Nova holds: as the leading-edge nodes (3nm, 2nm, gate-all-around) ramp through 2026, Nova captures content via integrated metrology, and the geographic mix is rotating toward higher-quality (Taiwan) revenue as China visibility softens. No evidence of share loss at any leading foundry customer.

### semiconductor_cycle

- **Status:** Confirm
- **Evidence:** Q4 revenue +14% Y/Y, FY +31% Y/Y, with management projecting WFE growth at low-double-digits in 2026 and Nova outperforming. The Mani (BofA) Q&A explicitly mapped growth vectors across DRAM, foundry, and logic, with management positive across all three. Working capital position ($1.6B in cash and equivalents at year-end, $218M FCF generated in 2025) confirms the company is operating in a cycle upturn, not a downturn.
- **Implication:** Nova is riding the front edge of an upcycle that broadens beyond AI-driven logic into DRAM and advanced packaging. The +31% FY'25 print on top of difficult 2024 comps, combined with confidence in continued 2026 growth, supports the thesis that the semi cycle for metrology specifically is mid-cycle, not late-cycle. Risk to watch: any sharp WFE consensus reset would compress the magnitude of Nova outperformance even if the share story remains intact.

### lithography_roadmap

- **Status:** Confirm (with nuance — Nova plays adjacent to lithography, not in it)
- **Evidence:** Nova's metrology roadmap is explicitly tied to lithography-driven complexity at gate-all-around, CFET, and advanced memory nodes. Management cited "advancing its technology offerings through R&D investments, focusing on emerging challenges such as gate-all-around, CFET, and advanced memory" and a "new metrology solution that combines optical and materials metrology with advanced modeling and signal analysis capabilities." The ELIPSON and METRION platforms were called out as transitioning from R&D qualification to high-volume manufacturing. Critical dimension/film metrology share at 25% with continued share gains.
- **Implication:** The lithography roadmap thesis is best read for Nova as a derivative play — every node transition (EUV → high-NA EUV, gate-all-around → CFET) drives metrology intensity. Nova's positioning at all four leading-edge logic players means the company captures share regardless of which lithography vendor wins each pocket. Thesis is intact and arguably strengthening as advanced packaging (hybrid bonding, HBM) adds a second-axis content vector independent of lithography node migration.

## 8. Management Q&A flags

1. **Charles Shi (Needham) — gross margin sensitivity to memory pricing.** Shi asked whether rising memory prices are flowing through to Nova's gross margin. Waisman gave a firm "memory pricing does not correlate with Nova's gross margins" — clean answer, no dodge. Useful signal that Nova's margin model is mix-driven (logic vs memory vs packaging), not pass-through to customer pricing.

2. **Vedvati Shrotre (Evercore) — lead times and optical component constraints.** Shrotre asked specifically about optical component supply (a known sector pinch point). Waisman acknowledged "pressure on lead times" but said optical components specifically are NOT the driver — attributed lead time pressure to customer urgency. This is a slightly defensive answer; analyst was probing for a specific bottleneck and management deflected without giving a quantified picture of how lead times have moved. Worth probing on next call.

3. **Yiling Sun (Citi, for Atif Malik) — gate-all-around accumulated revenue.** Direct ask: does cumulative gate-all-around revenue exceed $500M by 2026? Waisman confirmed "on track to meet" the target. This is unusually specific — Nova has been tracking a public cumulative revenue number for GAA, which is a useful future verification point and suggests management is comfortable signaling confidence in the GAA ramp.

4. **Matthew Prisco (Cantor) — Has customer dialogue translated into actual H2'26 orders?** Waisman gave a generally optimistic framing across multiple growth vectors but did not commit to a hard orders-in-hand number for H2'26. This is appropriate at this stage (a Feb call commenting on H2 visibility), but a flag to revisit: if Q1'26 doesn't show H2 order conversion clearly, the H2 acceleration narrative will need defense.

5. **Shane Brett (Morgan Stanley) — advanced packaging growth moderation.** Brett probed the expected moderation in advanced packaging growth (since it grew 60%+ in 2025). Waisman explained the moderation as a natural function of scale/comp difficulty rather than a market slowdown — and framed continued "double-digit" advanced packaging growth in 2026. The honest acknowledgment of moderation is a positive credibility signal; sandbagging would have been to project continued 60% growth.
