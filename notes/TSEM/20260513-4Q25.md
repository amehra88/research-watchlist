## 3. Forward guidance

- **Q1'26 revenue:** $412M midpoint, +15% YoY vs Q1'25. (Implied sequential decline from Q4'25's $440M, typical seasonal pattern; the YoY framing is what management emphasized.)
- **2028 target model (new):** $2.84B revenue (22% CAGR from 2025); $750M net profit (50% CAGR — implying material margin lift); 26.4% net margin; ~39.4% gross margin; ~31.7% operating margin; 85% utilization across owned capacity *excluding Intel Fab 11X*.
- **CapEx plan:** $920M total committed ($650M previously announced + $270M new this quarter), 28% spent by end of 2025, balance through 2026-2027. Management explicitly stated no additional CapEx needed beyond this to hit the 2028 model.
- **Guidance philosophy shift:** point-estimate Q1 guide combined with a multi-year financial model is more ambitious framing than typical for TSEM. The 2028 model is anchored on already-reserved capacity (>70% of incremental SiPho capacity reserved through 2028, with customer prepayments) which is meaningfully credibility-enhancing — this is a guidance-by-backlog construction, not a guidance-by-hope construction.
- **Tone:** Confident, operationally specific. Risks acknowledged (memory shortages affecting mobile, minor capacity-qualification timing slippage risk, Intel Fab 11X mediation) but compartmentalized.

## 4. Thesis read by theme

### foundry_capacity

- **Status:** Confirm (with positive drift toward higher-value mix)
- **Evidence:** "$920M CapEx plan ... an additional $270M ... on top of the previously announced $650M, to further expand its capacity ... expansion is expected to yield capacity growth greater than 5x of the actual fourth-quarter monthly wafer shipments of Silicon Photonics. A significant portion of this capacity is already reserved through 2028, with customer prepayments." Fab utilization mix: Fab 2 60%, Fab 3 85%, Fab 7 100% (Q4'25). New tool installation through mid-Q3'26, full qualification by December 2026 (prepared remarks / Q&A with Tavy Rosner, Barclays).
- **Implication:** Foundry capacity thesis is being executed on, and the value-per-wafer mix is shifting up (SiPho, SiGe, RF SOI) while lower-margin RF Mobile is deliberately deprioritized (-15% YoY in 2025). The 5x SiPho capacity expansion with pre-funded customer commitments is a structurally different supply story than a generic capacity build — it's contracted growth, not speculative. Confirms the thesis and arguably strengthens it.

### semiconductor_cycle

- **Status:** Confirm
- **Evidence:** Q4'25 revenue +14% YoY, FY'25 +9% YoY, Q1'26 guide +15% YoY. Power Management +20% YoY (16% of revenue). 300mm RF SOI +5.5%. Three of top four Tier-1 RF front-end module providers won in 2025, with revenue ramps in 2027-2028. Management commentary describes broad-based demand strength with no inventory normalization caveats.
- **Implication:** TSEM is benefiting cleanly from the upcycle, and the company-specific mix shift (AI/optical content gain) is layered on top of the underlying cycle tailwind. The "cycle" lens may be becoming less informative for TSEM — increasingly the story is AI/optical content gain in a specialty-foundry niche rather than cyclical recovery. Worth considering whether ai_infrastructure_capex or ai_compute_topology should be added as a theme (see Vocabulary candidates section).

### china_us_tensions

- **Status:** Drift (mostly absent from this call — limited new information)
- **Evidence:** No direct discussion of China export controls, China revenue exposure, or US tariff exposure in the available transcript summary. Intel Fab 11X situation (US-based fab originally agreed with Intel, Intel opting out Sept 2025, flows redirected to Fab 7 Japan) is the closest adjacent geopolitical signal — an indirect read-through that Tower's US capacity strategy via the Intel agreement is dead and the company is concentrated on Israel + Japan + Italy + Texas (legacy) instead. Currency hedging discussion mentions yen and shekel exposure (natural hedges + zero-cost cylinders).
- **Implication:** The china_us_tensions theme isn't directly stress-tested by this call. The deeper structural drift is that TSEM's US manufacturing footprint plan has materially shrunk (Fab 11X opt-out), which may matter for any future US-content / CHIPS-related opportunity but isn't a thesis-breaker. Consider whether this theme remains the right framing for TSEM or whether geographic_concentration_risk / US_manufacturing_footprint would be a more precise lens given the Fab 11X development.

## 5. AI positioning signal

- **Current score:** unpopulated in watchlist
- **Evidence from this call:** Direct and material AI exposure now disclosed. Silicon Photonics revenue $228M in 2025 (up from $106M in 2024 — 115% growth, now 27% of total revenue). Stated role as a leading supplier for 1.6T Silicon PICs. Active Nvidia collaboration (Nvidia ships through module customers, not directly from Tower, but Tower provides the technology and manufacturing for transceiver-equivalent modules). Engaged with XPU makers on co-packaged optics (CPO) strategies. Working on 400G-per-lane next-gen optics. SiPho capacity to grow 5x by late 2026 with >70% reserved through 2028.
- **Recommendation:** initiate at **4** (on the 1-5 scale used elsewhere in watchlist)
- **Reasoning:** TSEM is now a real AI infrastructure beneficiary via a specific, defensible position (specialty foundry for SiPho and SiGe used in 800G/1.6T optical transceivers — a chokepoint for hyperscaler back-end network buildout). The Nvidia attribution is concrete. Not a 4+ or 5 because TSEM is one step removed from the GPU/XPU layer and faces eventual competition from larger foundries if SiPho volumes scale enough to attract them. Score of 4 reflects "material direct AI exposure with clear defensible niche, but not a CUDA-style moat." PM should confirm before this is added to watchlist.

## 6. Competitive advantage signal

- **Innovation rate** (current: unpopulated)
  - Evidence: 400G-per-lane and CPO development underway; SiPho design wins with hyperscaler module makers; 3 of top 4 Tier-1 RF FEM vendors won in 2025; deliberate portfolio shift up the value stack (away from RF Mobile, toward optical/RF SOI/Power Management).
  - Recommendation: initiate at **4**. Tower is innovating in a specific, valuable specialty-foundry lane — not at the leading edge (no 3nm work), but at the level relevant for its served market.
- **Distribution** (current: unpopulated)
  - Evidence: Customer prepayments locking up >70% of incremental SiPho capacity through 2028. Module-maker customer base that supplies Nvidia. Geographic spread (Israel, Japan, Italy, Texas) is largely a manufacturing footprint, not a customer-distribution moat.
  - Recommendation: initiate at **3+**. Strong with specific large customers (concentrated, contracted relationships), weaker on breadth. The prepayment-backed capacity reservation is unusual and a real positive signal on customer commitment, but it also concentrates risk.
- **Overall** (current: unpopulated)
  - Recommendation: initiate at **4-**
  - Reasoning: Not a mechanical average. Innovation rate is solid in the niche; distribution is narrow but deep — and notably for a foundry, distribution = customer book quality, not geographic reach. The customer prepayment model materially de-risks the capital plan, which is the most important distribution signal for a capacity-heavy business. Offsetting: customer concentration risk is real (loss of one major SiPho customer would be very painful), and the Intel Fab 11X collapse is a recent reminder that big customer agreements can break. 4- captures "stronger than the foundry-tier average for its niche, weaker than the bellwether (TSM) on diversification."

## 8. Management Q&A flags

1. **Nvidia commercial relationship — careful framing.** Hosseini (Susquehanna) and Rosner (Barclays) both probed the Nvidia relationship. Ellwanger was precise: Tower does **not** ship directly to Nvidia. Tower supplies modules to module-maker customers who then sell to Nvidia. This is a meaningful nuance — the headline "Nvidia partner" framing investors will run with is one degree removed from a direct supply relationship. Not a dodge, but a clarification worth flagging for sizing purposes.
2. **2028 model — "target" vs. "run-rate."** Anderson (Craig-Hallum, in for Richard) pressed on whether the 2028 model is a full-year or exit-rate number. Ellwanger said "achievable within 2028, with aspirations for it to represent full-year figures." That's a soft answer — full-year is the aspiration, but the run-rate interpretation is the fallback. PM should treat the $2.84B / $750M as a Q4'28 run-rate construct, not a definitive FY28 P&L.
3. **Intel Fab 11X — mediation, not litigation, not resolution.** Management compartmentalized this well but the mediation is ongoing. Material question: any settlement liability or asset write-down risk? Not addressed in the available summary. Worth tracking via 6-K filings (multiple 6-Ks already filed in 2026 — see Sourcing).
4. **Memory shortages and mobile.** Acree (Benchmark) flagged memory shortage / price risk to handset demand. Ellwanger acknowledged but described mitigation. Worth flagging because RF Mobile is being deprioritized anyway — if mobile weakness becomes acute, TSEM has cover via mix shift, but it would slow the gross margin walk to the 2028 target.
5. **Power business — no 800V platform yet.** Hosseini asked about AI server rack high-voltage capability. Ellwanger acknowledged Tower doesn't have an 800V platform, just existing high-voltage ICs. This is a capability gap relative to the data-center power buildout story — worth noting but probably not material to thesis at current scale.
