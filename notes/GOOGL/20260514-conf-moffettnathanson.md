# GOOGL — MoffettNathanson Media Internet and Communications Conference read

_Prepared by earnings-reviewer-from-pdf (offline PDF processing), 2026-05-21. Tier: T1 (BCTK). Source: uploaded conference transcript (FactSet CallStreet PDF, 14-May-2026, 15 pages). Speaker: Neal Mohan, CEO of YouTube (Alphabet). Format: ~40-minute fireside chat with Michael Nathanson (third Mohan appearance in four years). Position in our record: occurs 15 days after the 1Q26 print (2026-04-29) and one day after YouTube's annual Brandcast Upfront (2026-05-13). This is the first YouTube-specific event in our notes record — all prior GOOGL notes covered consolidated earnings or CFO-level conferences._

## 1. Headline read

YouTube-only fireside that **reinforces the YouTube-as-flywheel thesis** without surfacing new quantitative disclosures beyond the 1Q26 print. Mohan's framing is consistent and on-message: creators → 2B daily viewers (200mm hours/day in US living room) → monetization (AVOD + one-third SVOD). Notable substantive disclosures: (1) **YouTube Music + Premium "over 125 million subscribers"** (vs. the consolidated 350mm paid consumer subs disclosed at 1Q26), (2) **first Premium price increase in three years** just executed, (3) **YouTube Shorts RPM at parity with long-form in several countries including the US, exceeding it in some**, (4) **Ask Studio (creator AI) and Ask (viewer AI) at 75mm regular users in April**, (5) **>$100B paid to creator economy over last four years**. The note acknowledges the YouTube ad-growth deceleration question (Nathanson Q on p.11) but Mohan's answer stays at the framework level — no quantitative defense of the YouTube +11% Q1 number. Single conference signal, weaker than an earnings print and weaker still because Mohan is a divisional CEO not the Alphabet CFO — but the tape is uniformly reinforcing of standing theses, and the Shorts-RPM-parity disclosure is the cleanest new monetization-progress signal in our record.

## 2. Strategic themes management emphasized

1. **YouTube flywheel: creators → viewers → monetization, with the creator as the unique distinctive asset.** "Creators, YouTubers call YouTube their home. That's differentiated from social media. It's differentiated from streaming and TV" (p.3). The flywheel framing is identical to prior Mohan appearances and is the operative model.

2. **Long-term bet discipline as a structural advantage.** Mohan invokes three big bets that took years to pay off: (a) YouTube Music + Premium subscriptions ("people thought we were crazy"); (b) YouTube in the living room ("over 50% of watch time happens on TV screens in the US"); (c) podcasts (now "number one podcast platform globally"). The "long-bet" framing is being institutionalized as the YouTube competitive moat narrative (p.4).

3. **Premium subscription business hits its strongest quarter and gets first price increase in three years.** "This was our strongest quarter ever in Q1" (p.5). "First time in three years that we raised price on Premium just a few weeks ago" (p.5). YouTube TV also has "10 different tiers, sports bundle, sports plus news, entertainment, et cetera" — TAM-expanding via consumer choice (p.5). YouTube Music + Premium has "over 125 million subscribers" (p.4). Subscription business growing at "literally high-teens rates" per Nathanson's framing (p.4) — note that GOOGL has not publicly disclosed this growth rate.

4. **AI is empowering, not replacing, human creativity across all three audiences (creators, viewers, advertisers).** Creator-side: AI insertion tools, Ask Studio (data assistant). Viewer-side: improved Gemini-powered recommendations; Ask feature embedded under videos with 75mm regular users in April. Advertiser-side: AI-driven creative generation, campaign optimization (p.6-7). Mohan: "AI is, first and foremost, about making our creators' jobs easier and more effective" (p.6).

5. **AI slop defense via long-term-satisfaction recommendation systems.** "The way that our recommendation systems on YouTube work is looking at a concept of long-term satisfaction... not just watch time in the near term, but are you satisfied over the long term... AI slop is not something that you're going to see prevalence of on YouTube" (pp.8-9). Mohan also makes the counter-point that AI-driven creativity should not be over-suppressed ("watching live streams of people playing Minecraft... resulted in a $300 million grossing Minecraft movie last year").

6. **Creator economy investment and lock-in: $100B+ paid out over four years, 3 million monetizing creators.** "In the last four years, up to last year, we paid out over $100 billion to the creator economy" (p.10). "3 million creators in our YouTube Partner Program that monetize every single day" (p.10). New monetization rails: channel memberships, Paid Digital Goods, gifting, alongside AVOD and SVOD. The MrBeast "YouTube-first" anecdote (sign in his offices) reinforces the creator-stickiness narrative against competing platforms.

7. **Live and premium events as cultural-moment / water-cooler driver.** Sunday Ticket (NFL), Oscars in 2029, Coachella livestream, Artemis launch are framed as TAM-expanding + technology-differentiated (multiview) + creator-amplified (p.11-12). The strategic frame is consistent with the broader Alphabet positioning that live is a relatively scarce supply of cultural moments.

8. **Connected TV scale: 1B+ hours/day living room watch time globally, 200mm in US.** Demographics "across the board" (p.12). Most living-room content is "from our creators, from YouTubers" — i.e., the YouTube DNA is what is colonizing the TV, not licensed third-party content. Strategic implication: YouTube on living room is structurally different from Netflix/Disney/Max (creator-content vs. studio-content).

9. **Shorts: RPM parity reached in US + several countries; some countries exceed long-form.** "We've reached parity, not just in the US, but in several countries, and in some countries we've even exceeded it, including the US" (p.13). **This is the cleanest new monetization-progress disclosure in this conference.** Shopping stickers + 500,000 creators tagging shopping content is the secondary lever.

10. **Aggregator vision for YouTube TV.** Mohan reaffirms the "super aggregator" framing — primetime channels alongside endemic creator content, expanding into more countries — without committing to specific consolidation timelines (p.14-15).

## 3. Forward-looking commentary

- **Subscription business continues to add subs at record pace.** "This was our strongest quarter ever in Q1" for non-trial Premium subscribers globally and in the US (p.4). No quantitative forward guide given — consistent with conference rules.
- **YouTube TV 10-tier launch is "really early, probably too early to say anything concrete about it"** (p.14). Sets the expectation that the bundle-effectiveness conversation moves to 2H 2026 prints.
- **Premium pricing now an active lever after a three-year pause.** No commitment on cadence, but the principle is established: "if you're not delivering that value, you don't have a right to raise prices. So, that's the biggest thing... we all as consumers get to vote with our feet, so to speak."
- **Shorts monetization headroom continues to be incremental.** RPM has reached parity in the US; "delivering creator and viewer value first" reads as continued reinvestment posture rather than maximization (p.13). Implication: monetization upside from Shorts is real but management will lead with engagement.
- **Live event appetite confirmed forward.** Oscars 2029 + "you should expect to see [creator amplification] in the context of something like the Oscars" (p.12). Implies further rights bidding is on the table where TAM-expanding criteria are met.
- **Super-aggregator roadmap continues.** "We have many, many primetime channel partners now. We're in a handful of countries. We want to roll that out to more countries" (p.14). Geographic expansion is the operational lever, not new format invention.
- **Next earnings:** Q2 2026 print confirmed for **2026-07-23** (InsiderScore `future_earnings_dates`, queried 2026-05-21; market cap $4.696T at query time).
- **Tone:** Confident, on-message, no sandbagging. Mohan handles the deceleration question on p.11 with framework-level discipline ("ultimately, what is in the best interest of our advertisers? What are they telling us?") rather than defensiveness or quantitative redirection.

## 8. Q&A or fireside-chat flags

1. **Non-defensive answer on YouTube ad deceleration vs. Search (Nathanson Q, p.11).** Mohan stays at the ROI / framework level and does not push back on the framing that YouTube is decelerating while Search is accelerating. **This is the cleanest soft acknowledgment in our record that YouTube +11% lagging Search +19% is recognized as the operative narrative.** PM should track the YouTube ad-growth trajectory in the Q2 2026 print specifically — if YouTube reaccelerates back toward mid-teens, the divergence narrative resolves favorably; if it stays at ~11% while Search holds high-teens, it becomes a structural watch item.

2. **First Premium price increase in three years (Nathanson Q, p.5).** New pricing posture is now active. Mohan: "if you're not delivering that value, you don't have a right to raise prices." Notable that this is happening alongside "the most non-trial subscribers ever in the history of Premium globally, but also in the US" (p.4) — both signals point to consumer subscription pricing power. Watch Q2 print for explicit ARPU disclosure or YouTube subscription revenue commentary.

3. **Shorts RPM at parity with long-form in US + several countries (Nathanson Q, p.13).** "We've reached parity, not just in the US, but in several countries, and in some countries we've even exceeded it, including the US." **First explicit disclosure of Shorts RPM crossing long-form parity in the US in our record.** Material for the YouTube monetization durability thesis — Shorts had been the structural drag on blended YouTube monetization since 2022; that drag is now over in the US and meaningfully receding globally. Watch for any Ashkenazi corroboration in Q2 print.

4. **Ask (viewer) at 75mm regular April users (p.7).** Mohan: "just in April, that had 75 million users using it on a regular basis." First time we see a Gemini-or-Gemini-adjacent consumer-AI MAU number outside the consolidated 350mm paid-subs framing from 1Q26. PM should track whether Ask MAU compounds quarter-over-quarter in Q2 disclosure — if it does, it becomes a sub-metric for the consumer-AI engagement narrative independent of paid subscriptions.

5. **Non-endemic creator trend (Mohan, p.3-4).** Dwyane Wade, Draymond Green, Trevor Noah, Oprah explicitly cited as wanting to become YouTubers. **The traditional-celebrity migration to YouTube is the cleanest structural-validation signal we have seen for the creator economy moat.** Read-through is competitive-positive for YouTube vs. all other media platforms (linear TV, streaming, social). Diagnostic phrase for future tracking: "non-endemic creator."

6. **Markiplier theatrical release as creator-economy proof point (p.4).** Mohan: "Markiplier... self-funded [a movie]... released it in theaters... opened to thousands of theaters and was the number one grossing movie on the weekend that it was released." Single anecdote but reinforces the off-platform-success-as-on-platform-success framing — bullish for the creator-stickiness thesis vs. competing platforms trying to poach YouTube creators.

7. **MrBeast "YouTube-first" rule (p.10).** Mohan: "You go into his offices, there's a sign that says the first rule of MrBeast is YouTube-first." Largest creator on the platform institutionally committed to YouTube as primary distribution. Read-through to competitor poaching attempts: blunted.

8. **Aggregator question soft-defer (Nathanson Q, p.14).** Nathanson asks if YouTube TV will become the super-aggregator for premium content. Mohan: pointed to primetime channels rollout in more countries, did not commit to specific consolidation timeline. PM should treat the super-aggregator vision as a 2027+ catalyst, not 2026.
