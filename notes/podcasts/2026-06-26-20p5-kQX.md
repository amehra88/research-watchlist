# What does the next training paradigm look like?

Here are the key investment insights from the podcast episode.

### Core Investment Thesis: The Primacy of Scale and Compute

The central bet of major AI labs is that current model limitations (e.g., data inefficiency, lack of continuous learning) can be "steamrolled" by massively scaling up training compute. This is analogous to how Large Language Models (LLMs) solved many long-standing problems in natural language processing simply by throwing more compute at them.

This implies a multi-year, secular trend of massive capital expenditure on compute infrastructure. The speaker speculates that spending on Reinforcement Learning (RL) environments could grow from billions to a trillion dollars. This is a strong bullish signal for the entire AI supply chain, particularly semiconductor companies (e.g., NVDA, TSM) and the hyperscalers building out the infrastructure (e.g., MSFT, GOOGL, AMZN).

### Key Sector Trend: "Grindability" as a Bottleneck

A critical factor for AI progress in any domain is not just whether a task's success is *verifiable*, but whether it is *grindable*. Grindability is the ability to run thousands of parallel, deterministic, and replayable simulations of a task.

-   **High Grindability Domains (Faster Progress):** Coding and games are highly grindable because they can be perfectly simulated in self-contained environments. This explains the rapid progress of AI in these areas.
-   **Low Grindability Domains (Slower Progress):** Real-world computer use (e.g., navigating Amazon's checkout, using enterprise software like Slack or Gmail) is not grindable. You cannot run thousands of parallel bots on a live website without being blocked. Progress for AI agents in these real-world, interactive domains will be significantly slower, which may temper near-term expectations for AI-driven productivity gains in the broader economy.

This creates a potential moat for large incumbents (e.g., GOOGL, MSFT, CRM, AMZN) who can create high-fidelity, sandboxed clones of their own applications for AI training—a feat difficult for startups to replicate.

### Key Sector Trend: Continuous vs. In-Context Learning

The inefficiency of "continuous learning"—updating a model's core weights based on real-world interaction—is a major challenge. Gradient updates are extremely sample-inefficient. This has led to two divergent paths with significant economic implications:

1.  **The "In-Context" Bet:** Many labs are betting that architectural innovations will lead to near-infinitely large context windows. If a model can hold an entire "on-the-job" learning experience (e.g., 6 months of employee onboarding) in its context, the need to update its core weights diminishes. This makes the base model a fixed, amortizable asset and shifts the recurring cost entirely to inference. This trend reinforces the massive, ongoing demand for inference compute, benefiting GPU makers and cloud providers.
2.  **The "Continuous Learning" Problem:** A significant portion of compute spend (30-50% for AI labs) is currently on inference, which does not improve the underlying model. This is described as a "huge waste." A breakthrough in efficiently feeding deployment learnings back into the model's weights ("on-policy self-distillation" or "dreaming") would be a paradigm shift, but the path there is unclear, especially for non-grindable, real-world tasks.

### Capital Allocation & Valuation Commentary

-   **Sustained, Massive CapEx:** The core thesis points to continued, aggressive capital spending by AI leaders. The primary beneficiaries are companies providing the foundational compute resources.
-   **Training vs. Inference Economics:** The high, upfront cost of model training is a one-time amortized cost. The ongoing, and potentially larger, cost is inference. Investors should model the long-term cost structures of AI companies based on inference demand, especially as context windows grow. The current inability to efficiently use inference data to improve models represents a massive, addressable inefficiency in the AI value chain.
