# AI benchmark scores don’t tell you what you think they do

### Core Investment Thesis: "Test-Time Compute" as a New Moat

The key insight is that AI model performance is not a static benchmark score. It is a direct function of the compute budget allocated at the time of inference ("test-time compute"). The same model (e.g., a GPT-class model) can perform significantly better if given a larger budget (e.g., $10,000 vs. $10) to "think" about a problem. This implies that the value of an AI service will depend not only on the underlying model but also on the provider's willingness and ability to spend on inference.

### Competitive Dynamics & Hyperscaler Advantage

This dynamic creates a significant competitive advantage for capital-rich companies, particularly the hyperscalers like Microsoft (MSFT), Google (GOOGL), and Amazon (AMZN).

*   **Durable Moat:** Companies with larger balance sheets can afford to allocate more compute per query, delivering a superior AI product from the exact same foundational model as a smaller competitor. This turns access to capital and efficient compute into a key differentiator and a durable moat.
*   **Performance vs. Cost Trade-off:** The market will likely see a tiering of AI services based on performance, which will be directly tied to inference cost. Enterprise customers may be willing to pay a premium for higher-quality outputs that require more compute.

### Capital Allocation Signals

*   **Inference Spend as a Leading Indicator:** Investors should view a company's investment in inference compute (either through capex for their own chips/datacenters or opex with cloud providers) as a direct investment in product quality. Increased guidance on compute spend could be a bullish signal for the competitiveness of their AI offerings.
*   **Example:** The discussion uses GPT-3 (an OpenAI model) as an example. The ability of its primary partner, Microsoft (MSFT), to fund massive-scale test-time compute is a core part of their competitive strategy against rivals.

### Regulatory & Risk Factors

*   **Inadequate Safety Frameworks:** Current AI safety policies ("preparedness frameworks") are flawed because they evaluate a model's capabilities in a static, low-compute environment. They do not account for the emergent, potentially dangerous capabilities that could be unlocked by applying a massive compute budget at inference time.
*   **Future Regulatory Risk:** This gap suggests that future regulations may need to address not just the training of models, but also the amount of compute that can be deployed for inference. This could impose new compliance costs or limitations on the most powerful AI applications.
