# The data black hole at the center of AI

Here are the investment-related insights from the podcast episode.

### Top Investable Insights

- **Data & Compute are the Primary Drivers, Not Efficiency:** Recent AI progress is not from making models smarter (more sample-efficient), but from feeding them dramatically more data and compute. Models are "ludicrously inefficient" learners, requiring a million-fold more data than a human to learn. This brute-force approach is a powerful tailwind for the entire AI infrastructure stack.

- **Bullish for Infrastructure (Compute & Data):** The massive data and compute requirements to overcome sample inefficiency are a direct benefit to GPU manufacturers (like NVIDIA) and the hyperscalers that provide AI training infrastructure (Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, Amazon AWS). The value chain for generating, labeling, and storing this data is projected to become a "deca-billion" dollar industry.

- **Model Moats May Be Weaker Than Perceived:** The primary driver of model performance is access to vast, high-quality datasets, not secret architectural tricks. This data advantage is vulnerable because model capabilities can be "distilled" by competitors querying public APIs to create their own training data. This suggests that the competitive moats of leading closed-model companies (OpenAI/Microsoft, Google, Anthropic/Amazon) may be less durable, potentially leading to commoditization over time.

### AI/Tech Sector Trends & Competitive Dynamics

- **Open Source is a Fast Follower:** Open-source models lag state-of-the-art closed models by only about 4 months. This is possible because data, the main driver of performance, can be replicated. This dynamic suggests a persistent and competitive open-source ecosystem will limit the long-term pricing power of frontier model providers.

- **Scaling Isn't a Silver Bullet:** Analysis of AI "scaling laws" suggests that simply making models bigger (adding more parameters) will not solve the fundamental problem of sample inefficiency. Even an infinitely large model would only reduce data requirements by a factor of ~10, a fraction of the thousands-to-millions-fold gap with human learning. This poses a long-term risk to the "scaling is all you need" paradigm and could cap the ROI on ever-larger training runs unless new architectures are developed.

- **High Costs for Real-World AI Applications:** The extreme data inefficiency of current AI is a major hurdle for complex, real-world applications like autonomous vehicles. Companies like Alphabet's Waymo (GOOGL) and Tesla (TSLA) need 3-4 orders of magnitude more data to train a self-driving model than a human needs to learn to drive. This implies sustained, high capital expenditure and R&D costs for companies tackling these challenges.

- **Labor Market Impact is Nuanced:** The host predicts that despite AI's capabilities, the demand for human software engineers will be *higher* in 2027 than today. This is because AI will act as a complementary tool, increasing the productivity of human engineers and expanding the scope of what can be built, rather than replacing them outright.
