# The Media Game Has Changed

Here are the top public market and AI investing insights from the podcast.

**Specific Public Company Mentions**

*   **Palantir (PLTR):** CEO Alex Karp is described as a master of "new media." His strategy is to frame the company's narrative by discussing broad, important world trends (geopolitics, the future of the US military, superintelligence) rather than the product itself. This "outside-in" approach is highlighted as a highly effective model for modern CEOs. His interviews are noted for almost never mentioning Palantir directly, instead associating him and the company with globally significant topics.

*   **NVIDIA (NVDA):** CEO Jensen Huang is praised as one of the best and most effective communicators in tech today, ranking him alongside Elon Musk and Alex Karp. This is a strong qualitative endorsement of his leadership and ability to control the company's narrative.

*   **Tesla (TSLA):** CEO Elon Musk is used as a prime example of the "CEO as the brand." The speakers note that when critics attack Musk's companies (like SpaceX), they are primarily attacking him personally, demonstrating how intertwined the leader and the company have become in the public perception.

**AI/Tech Sector Trends & Strategy**

*   **The CEO is the Brand:** In the current media environment, a company's brand is increasingly embodied by its leader. Companies that are "winning in marketing" have a CEO who is an authentic and compelling storyteller. For investors, this suggests that evaluating a CEO's ability to communicate directly and build a personal brand is a crucial part of due diligence.

*   **"New Media" is an Offensive Strategy:** Tech companies can no longer rely on traditional ("old") media to tell their story fairly. The old media model is described as defensive and reactive. The winning strategy is to "go direct" via podcasts, social media (like X/Twitter), and blogs. This allows a company to play offense by shaping its own narrative, building a direct relationship with its audience, and making itself "interesting."

*   **Storytelling: Outside-In vs. Inside-Out:** The most effective narrative strategy is "outside-in." Instead of focusing on company-specific news and product features ("inside-out"), leaders should connect their company to larger, more interesting trends happening in the world. This contextualizes the company's importance and captures a much broader audience.

*   **Authenticity Over Media Training:** Traditional, highly-polished media training that aims for "minimum controversy" is an outdated and ineffective model. The new media landscape rewards authenticity and a willingness to have real conversations in public, similar to how one would speak privately. Uninteresting or overly-sanitized messaging gets lost in the noise.
